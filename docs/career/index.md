# Career and communication

> Methods chapters teach you the work. This section helps you communicate it, fund it, and navigate the career around it.

Every other section of this handbook is about a craft: preprocessing, modelling, statistics, deployment. You can master all of them and still get rejected from journals, lose grants you should have won, and stall at career transitions you should have made. The skills below are the ones nobody examines you on, and nobody teaches you in graduate school.

This is a short section by design. It exists because the rest of the handbook would be misleading without it — implying that craft alone is the job.

## Why these three pages, and why now

Three artifacts dominate your visible output as a neuroimaging researcher:

1. **A methods section.** Read by reviewers, replicators, and your future self. Most are bad. The first page shows you how to write one that another lab could rerun.
2. **A grant.** Read by funders who pay for the next five years of your work. The second page covers funder landscape and the specific failure modes of imaging grants.
3. **A CV / resume / cover letter.** Read at the moments your career changes shape. The third page covers the four moves most often made out of (and into) neuroimaging.

Communication failures at any of these three points cost more than any single methods mistake.

## What this section is not

- **A guide to academic politics.** That's a book, not a handbook chapter, and the politics are local enough that generic advice is misleading.
- **A statistics or analysis-design chapter.** Power analysis lives in [Analysis → Task fMRI design and power](../analysis/design.md). This section will link to it, not duplicate it.
- **A data-engineering portfolio guide.** That lives in [Data engineering → Portfolio roadmap](../data-engineering/portfolio-roadmap.md), which already covers turning a Snakemake pipeline into an interview-ready artefact. The grant page links to it; the transitions page references it.
- **An academia-vs-industry crusade.** Both are reasonable destinations; the transitions page treats them as choices, not verdicts.

## Reading order

Top to bottom is fine — the pages don't depend on each other. If you're under deadline pressure, pick the one matching what you're writing this month.

## A note on voice

The advice here is opinionated. Anywhere a reasonable mentor would disagree, that disagreement is itself useful — it tells you to seek a second opinion from someone in your institution who has actually walked the path you're considering. Generic platitudes ("network!", "tell a story!") are deliberately absent.
