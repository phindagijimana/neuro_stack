# Psychiatric disorders

> Where neuroimaging is most ambitious and most humbling. Small effect sizes, large samples needed, and a decade of reckoning with reproducibility.

Imaging in psychiatry promised early on to deliver biomarkers, diagnoses, and treatment-selection tools. Three decades and tens of thousands of patient-scans later, the field has produced one truly robust population-level structural finding per disorder, a constellation of weaker functional findings, and — crucially — a methodological consensus that we were under-powered for almost everything we tried.

The 2022 Marek paper ([Marek et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-022-04492-9)) crystallised the reckoning: brain-wide association studies on typical samples of tens-to-hundreds produce inflated, irreproducible effects, and credible findings need *thousands*. This page covers what has survived that reckoning, by disorder, and where the next decade is going.

For the underlying functional-network and neuroanatomical vocabulary (DMN, salience, sgACC, amygdala, striatum), see [Fundamentals → Neuroscience & neurology](../fundamentals/foundations/neuroscience.md).

## Why psychiatric imaging is hard

Three structural problems:

1. **Small effect sizes.** True brain–behaviour effects in psychiatry are typically r ≈ 0.05–0.15 — orders of magnitude smaller than effects in neurology (a stroke lesion, a Parkinson's DAT deficit, an AD hippocampal atrophy).
2. **Diagnostic heterogeneity.** DSM categories are not neurobiological. Two patients with the same MDD diagnosis can have non-overlapping symptom profiles. Cross-diagnostic transdiagnostic approaches (RDoC, HiTOP) are an attempt to fix this.
3. **Cohort sizes that were too small.** Most papers from 2005–2018 used n=20–100 per group. Marek 2022 showed that this regime produces effects that don't replicate at all in independent cohorts.

The consequence: most "famous" psych-imaging findings from that era are now appropriately questioned. The few that survived — schizophrenia ventriculomegaly, ENIGMA cortical patterns, ABCD-scale developmental findings — used much larger samples or meta-analytic pooling.

## Major depression

The most studied psychiatric disorder in imaging.

### Robust population-level findings

- **Hippocampal volume reduction**, especially with chronicity and recurrence. Effect size small but replicated in ENIGMA-MDD ([Schmaal 2016](https://doi.org/10.1038/mp.2015.69)).
- **Subgenual anterior cingulate (sgACC) hyperactivity** — the [Mayberg 2005](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2005.02.014) finding that motivated sgACC deep brain stimulation for treatment-resistant depression.
- **Default-mode network hyperconnectivity** — increased within-DMN coupling, with rumination phenotypes.
- **Reduced fronto-limbic connectivity** between prefrontal regulatory regions and amygdala / striatum.

### Treatment-relevant imaging

- **sgACC DBS** for treatment-resistant depression ([Mayberg 2005](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2005.02.014)) — RCT results have been mixed; longer-term observational results are more positive.
- **TMS targeting** of left dorsolateral prefrontal cortex; functional-connectivity-guided targeting shows efficacy improvements over scalp-based targeting ([Cash 2021](https://doi.org/10.1001/jamapsychiatry.2020.3794)).
- **Ketamine** response biomarkers — early but DMN normalisation is a candidate.

## Schizophrenia

The clearest structural imaging signal in psychiatry — though still small at the individual level.

### Robust findings

- **Ventricular enlargement** — replicated since the 1970s. Larger lateral ventricles in patients than controls, present at first episode.
- **Whole-brain and grey-matter volume reduction** — a few percent.
- **Cortical thinning pattern** — [van Erp et al., 2018](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.biopsych.2018.04.023) — the ENIGMA Schizophrenia cortical study, n ≈ 4 500, established a robust pattern of thinning in frontal, temporal, and insular cortex with relative sparing of parieto-occipital regions. This is the canonical cross-site replicable structural map.
- **Subcortical volumes** — smaller hippocampus, amygdala, thalamus; larger pallidum.
- **White-matter integrity reduction** in ENIGMA-SZ DTI work.

### Functional findings

- **Aberrant salience-network engagement.**
- **Reduced task-based DLPFC activity during working memory.**
- **Hippocampal hyperactivity** (HFOs / glutamatergic dysregulation hypothesis).

### Clinical use

Imaging is not currently diagnostic in schizophrenia. It plays a role in *excluding* structural causes (tumour, AVM, white-matter disease) in atypical presentations.

## Bipolar disorder

- **White-matter abnormalities** — ENIGMA-BD DTI shows widespread reduced FA.
- **Subcortical volume changes** — smaller hippocampus, thalamus; larger lateral ventricles.
- **Amygdala reactivity** to emotional stimuli; reduced prefrontal regulation.
- Overlap with schizophrenia is substantial on imaging, mirroring the genetic overlap.

## Anxiety / PTSD

- **Amygdala hyper-reactivity** to threat-related stimuli — the most replicated functional finding.
- **Hippocampal volume reduction** in PTSD — present pre-trauma in twin studies, suggesting risk factor rather than consequence ([Gilbertson 2002](https://doi.org/10.1038/nn958)).
- **Reduced ventromedial PFC regulation** of amygdala.
- **Salience-network hyperactivity** during interoceptive tasks in anxiety.

## ADHD

- **Smaller subcortical volumes** — caudate, putamen, accumbens, amygdala, hippocampus — established in the ENIGMA-ADHD mega-analysis ([Hoogman 2017](https://doi.org/10.1016/S2215-0366(17)30049-4)), n ≈ 1 700 + controls.
- **Delayed cortical maturation trajectory** ([Shaw 2007](https://doi.org/10.1073/pnas.0707741104)) — ADHD children show delayed peak cortical thickness rather than reduced thickness.
- **Frontostriatal functional connectivity differences** — less robust than the structural findings.

## Autism spectrum disorder

- **Early brain over-growth** in first ~2 years of life, then normalisation or under-growth in adolescence ([Courchesne 2007](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2007.10.016)).
- **Functional findings** from ABIDE network analyses: hypo- and hyper-connectivity findings have been contradictory across studies — a textbook example of why large, harmonised cohorts matter.
- **High heterogeneity** — ASD is a behavioural definition over many biologies. Subtyping work is active but unsettled.
- **Caution on generalisation** — most cohorts are predominantly male, white, high-functioning, English-speaking; findings rarely generalise to under-represented subgroups.

## Substance-use disorder

- **Striatal dopamine system** — reduced D2 receptor availability ([Volkow 2009](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuropharm.2008.07.011)) on [<sup>11</sup>C]raclopride PET, robust across drugs (alcohol, cocaine, methamphetamine).
- **Reduced prefrontal control** of striatum during craving.
- **Insular activity** during interoceptive craving cues.
- **Smoking cessation** outcomes predicted by pre-quit cue-reactivity imaging — early evidence for individual-level prediction.

## The reproducibility reckoning

[Marek et al., 2022](https://doi.org/10.1038/s41586-022-04492-9) — **BWAS (brain-wide association studies) demand thousands.** Using ABCD (n ≈ 12 000) and UK Biobank (n ≈ 35 000) and HCP (n ≈ 1 200), they showed:

- Univariate brain-behaviour effects are real but tiny (r ≈ 0.05).
- Sample sizes of n ≈ 100 produce highly inflated effects and almost zero replication.
- Multi-variate / predictive-modelling approaches need similar sample-size scaling.
- Genome-wide-association-style sample sizes are required for credible BWAS findings.

Practical implications for any psych-imaging project:

1. Either work on a population-scale cohort (ABCD, UK Biobank, ENIGMA) or treat your single-site finding as preliminary.
2. Pre-registration and held-out test sets are now expected.
3. Effect size matters more than p-value; a tiny replicable effect is more useful than a big one-shot effect.
4. Cross-validation isn't enough — independent replication is the standard.

## Open questions

- **Transdiagnostic frameworks.** RDoC (Research Domain Criteria) and HiTOP (Hierarchical Taxonomy of Psychopathology) re-cut the space along dimensions (negative valence, reward, threat) rather than diagnostic categories. Imaging endophenotypes for *dimensions* may be more tractable than for *diagnoses*.
- **Individual prediction vs group differences.** Group-mean differences don't tell you whether a single patient will benefit from a drug. Personalised-medicine imaging in psychiatry is the long-term goal; current progress is modest.
- **Treatment selection.** Which patient responds to which antidepressant / antipsychotic / psychotherapy — TMS targeting and ketamine response are the most promising near-term wins.
- **Developmental trajectories.** ABCD will eventually let us identify pre-clinical psychiatric trajectories and the imaging changes that precede them.
- **Confound control.** Head motion, medication, socioeconomic factors, smoking, BMI — every psych-imaging study fights these. Harmonised confound modelling is unsettled.
- **Reproducibility beyond Marek.** What's the right minimum n for a given effect size? How should the field credit registered replications?
- **Generalisation across demographics.** Most cohorts are WEIRD (Western, educated, industrialised, rich, democratic). Diverse-cohort recruitment is a structural problem the field hasn't solved.
